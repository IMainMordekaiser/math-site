I wish all of you guys could understand Vietnamese right now. Like, I'll try to translate the section on Equivalent Exchange in FMA here for you all:

"... we have a mandatory law, considered the universal truth in alchemy - the Law of Equivalent Exchange. You cannot obtain anything without exchanging something else of equal value. It is the iron law, the core concept. Alchemy is Equivalent Exchange. Equivalent Exchange is Alchemy. This law is repeated numerous times, considered the foundation of all of alchemy, and furthermore, the living motto of characters. And as the story progresses, we also see characters "exchange equivalently" in their own way.

Izumi tried to get her child back, and lost the ability to give birth. 

Ed and Al tried to resurrect their mother, and lost their body, their family. 

Roy Mustang tried to change the country, and lost his eyes to gain a vision of the future (ok so this part I'm not too sure). 

Gluttony ate everything and was eventually swallowed by another.

Lust had big boobs—big boobs are bad (a Vietnamese inside joke of sorts)—but “Lust” here did not mean sexual lust, but rather bloodlust. Her actions were cruel and barbaric, and her death was just as cruel and barbaric.

Sloth was lazy and thus died through hard labor; but Sloth also encompassed laziness in seeking love, in maintaining ethics, and in searching for goodness, all leading to apathy, seeing everything as a nuisance, and in the end, living and dying in meaninglessness, emotionlessness, and tastelessness.

Envy was jealous of humanity, always trying to appear big, strong, and threatening to conceal the pitiful self within, and so he committed suicide in his true form, out of shame and inferiority.

Wrath was, well, wrathful, so in his death there was only emptiness. Ironically, he found peace in death—the death brought by the sun and the moon, symbols of God in alchemy, as if God were striking his alchemical faith down.

Pride believed in his own superiority, yet he continuously fell into traps, was humiliated, and resorted to all sorts of cowardly methods to survive—only to die anyway. He was then reborn as a normal human child, the very thing he once looked down on.

Greed believed he wanted everything and sacrificed everything for what he wanted.

Father tried to swallow God, so he was swallowed in return—drowned, and reminded of who he was and where he belonged. 

The entire journey—the rise and fall of characters—all abides by the Law of Equivalent Exchange. An eye for an eye. “oH mY GoD EqUiVaLeNt ExChAnGe OmG it sounds so cool, I gotta go tattoo ‘equivalent exchange’ on my peepee right this insta—” NO!

You have to realize that all of those “equivalent exchanges,” and others I haven’t explicitly mentioned, *always lead to suffering and misery.* It’s like in the *Iliad*: if you only repay violence with violence, an eye for an eye, an arm for an arm, then humanity would be no different from Hector’s corpse. If you really watched the show, you would see that it introduces the concept of “equivalent exchange” only to prove that it’s a load of crap. If it originally came with alchemy as a justification for human sacrifice, then how could it be a universal truth? It is dogma that needs to be broken, not a principle to live by. Throughout the story, every attempt by the characters to “exchange equivalently” pulls them deeper into a loop of objectifying and instrumentalizing others.

In trying to resurrect their mother, and in trying to create the Philosopher’s Stone, Ed, Al, and Father respectively, wholeheartedly believed in equivalent exchange, and ended up viewing others as mere combinations of substances, a heap of cells, and strings of information, disregarding intelligence, personality, emotion, drive, dreams—everything that makes people who they are. They disregard people’s souls. What happened to them, in the end, was that their actions were blasphemous, unethical, inhumane, and always accompanied by horrifying, deterring punishment.

Ignoring personality, identity, and the independent existence of others, and instead viewing them only in terms of bodily information, function, and exchange value—that is exactly how Father sees the people of Amestris, how capitalism sees you: value, benefit, TOOL. A viewpoint that considers humans no different from inanimate objects.

So the journey of Ed and Al is not the journey of two alchemical heroes on their way to becoming the King of Alchemy and spreading the gospel of Equivalent Exchange, but the journey of two kids trying to find their own philosophy of living—seeking knowledge through science, and meaning through love. Equivalent exchange is neither the key to the door nor the complete answer. You cannot exchange your daughter and your dog for your thesis. You cannot exchange human lives for the Philosopher’s Stone. You cannot exchange a bunch of compounds to create a new human. Alchemy and its methods are only tools for humans to learn about the world; humanity itself cannot be bound by the ideology of alchemy.

Which is why, in their journey, Ed and Al must witness wonders and miracles that completely defy the law of equivalent exchange. Equivalent exchange cannot create a new human—but a mother can. Equivalent exchange cannot restore their arms, legs, and bodies—but a young mechanic can. Equivalent exchange cannot bring their mother back—but mentors, friends, and comrades can become their new family..."
